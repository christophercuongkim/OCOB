UPC Report On Interdisciplinary Business Concentrations

5/26/2011

 

Since Fall 2010, the UPC has deliberated the issues related to the delivery and the administration of the business interdisciplinary concentrations (IDCs). The UPC has reached an impass where a faculty vote is needed to move forward with this issue. Our aims in conducting a faculty vote before the close of this academic year are:

 

- To inform the faculty and report on the work the UPC has completed

- To seek input from the entire faculty in guiding the UPC's priorities

- To bring closure to an issue that has consumed UPC's agenda since Fall

- To allow the UPC to start the next academic year with a clear mandate

 

Below is a brief outline of the issues UPC has faced (with supporting documents). We ask you to carefully consider these documents --”required readings”-- before you turn in your vote. The UPC considered a faculty forum to discuss this issue but we voted to move forward without a faculty forum because:

 

- The faculty have all the materials needed to vote. We can add nothing new.

- It may be more constructive if the faculty review the relevant information provided by all parties, discuss it among themselves, ask questions from their UPC representatives (UPC Chair) and faculty concerned, and vote their decision

- We are unable to come up with a format for the forum that would satisfy all concerned faculty. Any UPC proposed format may be labeled as “unusual”.

- Lack of faculty interest in such a forum given the end of the year demands

 

Brief Background: In preparation for AACSB maintenance of accreditation, the faculty in all business concentrations (accounting, finance, marketing, and management), were asked to undertake assessment and review of their concentration. While this is a straightforward task for the Areas, it proved to be a challenge for OCOB' interdisciplinary concentrations (IDCs) in Entrepreneurship (Ent) and International Business (IB). The crux of the problem is this: It is simple to hold Areas accountable for delivery and review of concentrations (see OCOB’s Undergraduate Curriculum Committee Presentation to Academic Senate’s Curriculum Appeals Committee). It is difficult to do the same for interdisciplinary concentrations because there exists no ad-hoc “interest group,” or formally “designated” faculty from across relevant concentrations that have taken ownership of these IDCs. In fact, no concentration review of the IDCs has ever taken place and no faculty can be held accountable for the delivery of a quality curriculum.  Moreover, our inquiries from Areas showed that most faculty are unwilling to step forward and participate in teaching, assessment, and review of these IDCs, which is an essential requirement for the review of interdisciplinary concentrations.

 

After extensive research and deliberations, consultation with the faculty, and input from Area Chairs, we recognized that these IDCs cannot serve their originally proposed functions in their current configuration. Hence, given lack of interest among the faculty, the UPC unanimously recommended that both IDCs be discontinued. Our report to the senate “RE: Report of the OCOB's Undergraduate Programs Committee to the Academic Senate,” documents the background an the issues we considered (The Q&A part answers most common questions in this regard).

 

The UPC's recommendation was approved by the Dean and the Academic Senate Curriculum Committee (ASCC) last December. However, this item was pulled from the “Consent Agenda” for full Senate discussion. Curricular items pulled from the agenda are relegated to the Academic Senate Curricular Appeals Committee (ASCAC) for a final decision. The UPC prepared a comprehensive report (see RE: Report of the OCOB's Undergraduate Programs Committee to the Academic Senate) detailing the historical background of our IDCs and the process it had followed to arrive at its recommendation. At the Senate hearing, the Dean also provided a statement (see Dean Christy’s Comments for Academic Senate Meeting) about the problems with the administration of IDCs.  We urge you to read the UPC report and the Dean's statement as these documents answers most questions that you may arise.

 

 

In February, the ASCAC and the Senate Chair indicated that it is best for Cal Poly and our college if our faculty can find an “internal solution” to the IDC issue. The Dean in consultation with the UPC and the Senate developed a suggested guideline for resolving the curricular issues that had led to UPC's recommendations (see Dean Christy’s Notes on Development, Management and Assessment of a Concentration Curriculum in the OCOB). The faculty interested in Entrepreneurship followed these guidelines and developed a proposal to revamp Entrepreneurship. The UPC approved their proposal and suggested minor modification to strengthen this IDC. The UPC has not received a proposal from the IB faculty interest group, as requested in the Dean's guidelines.

 

As required by the Dean's guidelines, the UPC considered all pertinent issues and provided a comprehensive recommendation for an “internal solution” to the IDC problems on May 8, 2011 (see RE: Closure of Interdisciplinary Concentrations ). The ASCAC chose to ignore our proposed approach to resolving these issues and instead reversed our faculty's wishes (see RE: Recommendations on the ENT and IB Concentrations in OCOB).

 

The reversal of the unanimous UPC vote, as well as the recommendations of the Dean and the Senate's own curriculum committee (ASCC) is an unwarranted intrusion into OCOB's curricular affairs and the UPC find the ASCAC decision to be unconstructive. We are disturbed by the fact that the Senate has no formal policy on discontinuation of concentrations (let alone IDCs) and that there exists no acceptable record of the Senate having approved these IDCs in the first place.

 

Given this background, we have concluded that a faculty vote is necessary to inform the UPC's decisions on how to move forward with the interdisciplinary concentration in international business. In short, we seek your wisdom on this matter and urge you to study the documents noted above so that you can make an informed contribution to OCOB's curricular decisions. Please respect the efforts the UPC members have put forth on your behalf and base your vote on information we have furnished and with the long-term interest of our college in mind.

 

Thank you,

 

UPC Members
